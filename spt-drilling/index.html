Geotechnical design of deep excavations

Montreal's built environment sits on a geological puzzle assembled by the Champlain Sea and glacial retreat, which left behind a mantle of sensitive Leda clay across the eastern lowlands and dense till across the West Island. Anyone who has broken ground in Hochelaga-Maisonneuve or navigated the excavation rules in Outremont knows that the soil column changes radically within half a city block. In our experience, the Standard Penetration Test remains the most reliable first look into this subsurface chaos because the blow count profile directly flags transitions between competent till and the soft, high-plasticity silts that historically triggered flow slides in the Nicolet region. When the borehole advances into the marine clay layer, drilling crews often feel the sampler drop under its own weight before the hammer strikes—a tactile warning that bearing capacity calculations need a second look. For sites where the clay exceeds ten meters in thickness, we frequently pair the SPT program with a CPT survey to cross-check the undrained shear strength profile without the disturbance inherent in the split-spoon drive.

In Montreal's sensitive clay belt, a three-blow difference in SPT N-value can mean the difference between a spread footing and a pile-supported foundation.

Service characteristics in Montreal

Montreal's freeze-thaw cycle puts the SPT procedure to a different kind of test. From late December through March, frozen crust layers up to two meters deep form across the plain, forcing drilling crews to pre-thaw the ground or abandon shallow hand-auger approaches altogether. The Saint Lawrence lowlands add humidity that keeps the fine-grained soils near saturation year-round, so hammer energy transmission behaves very differently than in the drier till deposits of the Canadian Shield just north of Laval. Our field technicians calibrate the rope-and-cathead lift before every borehole because we have seen N-values drift by twenty percent when the hammer release mechanism picks up rust or mud from the previous shift. Downhole SPT data in the Montreal region feeds directly into the NBCC bearing resistance tables, but we also run companion grain-size analysis on the split-spoon samples to confirm whether the sand layers identified at depth are clean enough to serve as a bearing stratum or require a deeper pile option.

Critical ground factors in Montreal

The most persistent mistake we see in Montreal is a geotechnical report that treats N-values from the central plateau the same as those from the former river channels that snake beneath Griffintown. A contractor who assumes a blanket N=15 will design a footing that sits happily on stiff silt for nine months of the year, then watch differential settlement appear after the spring thaw saturates the underlying clay and cuts the effective stress in half. The NBCC explicitly requires that SPT-based bearing resistance factors account for the sensitivity of Champlain Sea deposits, and ignoring that clause has led to litigation over cracked slabs in more than one mid-rise project east of the downtown core. Even within a single city lot, we have logged N-values that drop from 22 to 4 across a vertical distance of less than two meters, which is why the CSA A23.3 commentary insists on conservative interpolation only when the borehole log demonstrates a steady trend—not a sudden collapse.

Our services

Our Montreal SPT program covers the full chain from borehole layout through lab scheduling, so the data that reaches the structural engineer has already been filtered for sample recovery anomalies and energy ratio corrections. Every field log includes the drill rig type, hammer weight, and drop height, because NBCC Division B treats those variables as non-negotiable inputs to the N60 conversion.

Subsurface Investigation with SPT

Truck-mounted or track-mounted hollow-stem auger drilling across Montreal Island, Laval, and the South Shore. Continuous split-spoon sampling at 1.5 m intervals, with reduced spacing in the upper sensitive clay zone as prescribed by BNQ 2501-135.

N60 Energy Correction and Liquefaction Screening

Hammer energy measurement using instrumented rods on the first borehole of each site. Corrected N60 values are plotted against depth and cross-referenced with the Seed-Idriss simplified procedure for liquefaction potential where sand layers are present below the water table.

Bearing Capacity and Settlement Estimates

Direct application of NBCC bearing resistance tables to SPT N60 profiles. Settlement predictions use the Burland and Burbridge method for granular soils and the Janbu tangent modulus approach for the Champlain clay sequence.

Frequently asked questions

How much does an SPT investigation cost for a standard residential lot in Montreal?

For a typical single-family lot on the island, a two-borehole SPT program with depth to 10 meters and a factual report generally falls between CA$810 and CA$900 per borehole, depending on access constraints and whether frozen ground requires pre-heating during the winter months. The total includes the drilling crew, split-spoon sampling, N60 correction, and the geotechnical data report.

What is the minimum number of SPT boreholes required for a Montreal building permit?

The boroughs generally defer to the geotechnical engineer's professional judgment under the Quebec Engineers Act, but for a typical triplex or small commercial structure, the NBCC commentary suggests a minimum of two boreholes spaced to capture the variability of the Champlain clay. Sites bordering the Lachine Canal or built on known fill may require a third borehole to satisfy the borough's excavation and shoring review.

Can SPT data alone confirm whether a Montreal soil is sensitive clay?

The SPT blow count can signal low strength—N-values below four in the marine clay band are a red flag—but sensitivity classification requires lab index testing. We routinely send a thin-walled Shelby tube sample from the same borehole to measure remolded shear strength via the fall cone method, which is the only way to confirm a sensitivity ratio above eight and trigger the NBCC's special precautions for flow-slide-prone deposits.

How does Montreal's winter affect SPT drilling schedules?

From January through mid-March, frost penetration in the Montreal plain can exceed 1.8 meters, which makes hollow-stem augering impractical without a pre-auger heater or a steam lance. We schedule SPT programs in exposed clay sites for late autumn or early spring to avoid the frozen crust, and in paved urban lots we can often drill through the asphalt and base course without weather-related delays.
