Montreal sits at roughly 45.5°N, where winter frost can penetrate 1.5 m into the ground. That alone changes soil resistivity seasonally. Our team uses Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES) to map subsurface layers without disturbing the pavement or buried utilities common across the island. The method injects a controlled DC current into the ground through stainless steel electrodes and measures the resulting potential difference. By progressively expanding the electrode spacing, the current probes deeper, building a resistivity-versus-depth profile. This works well in Montreal’s variable overburden — from the marine clays of the St. Lawrence Lowlands to the glacial till draped over the Canadian Shield. Before intrusive drilling, a CPT test can verify the transitions that resistivity first identifies, especially where clay thickness changes abruptly between Ville-Marie and the Plateau.

A 2D resistivity profile can pinpoint the clay-limestone interface to within half a metre before a single borehole is drilled.

Service characteristics in Montreal

A recent project near the Lachine Canal illustrates why VES is so practical here. The site was a former industrial lot with fill of unknown composition. Drilling would have risked hitting old foundations or contaminated lenses. The field crew laid out a Schlumberger array across 200 m, taking readings at 18 electrode spacings. The inverted resistivity profile revealed three distinct layers: a high-resistivity dry fill cap, a conductive clay band typical of the Champlain Sea deposits, and a resistive basement at 12 m depth — the top of the Trenton limestone. That profile matched historical borehole logs from the area. In Montreal, where bedrock can dip suddenly, this kind of non-intrusive reconnaissance saves time and avoids surprises. When bedrock is shallow, we often pair the survey with seismic refraction to map the rock surface continuously along a transect.

Critical ground factors in Montreal

The National Building Code of Canada (NBCC 2020) and CSA A23.3 require that foundation design accounts for actual ground conditions. In Montreal, a key risk is undetected soft clay lenses within the Champlain Sea sequence. These clays are normally consolidated and sensitive; if a resistivity survey misses a thick pocket because of poor electrode coupling on asphalt, the designer may assume uniform conditions where none exist. The consequence can be differential settlement under a raft or pile group. ASTM D6431-18 guides the field procedure, but local practice matters more: electrodes must be watered with saline solution in dry summer conditions to maintain contact resistance below 2 kΩ. Our team checks contact resistance at every station before injecting current, which is critical on the compacted granular fills that cover much of the island’s former industrial corridor.

Our services

We deliver three configurations of the electrical resistivity method, adapted to Montreal’s urban constraints and typical project scales.

1D Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES)

A single-point depth investigation using expanding Schlumberger array. Ideal for determining depth to bedrock below a proposed footing location. We interpret the sounding curve with iterative inversion software and correlate it with available borehole data from the Montreal area.

2D Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT)

A multi-electrode profile along a survey line. We space electrodes every 3 to 5 m and collect hundreds of data points per line. The result is a continuous cross-section showing lateral and vertical resistivity changes. Useful for mapping buried channels or contamination plumes.

Time-Lapse Resistivity Monitoring

Repeated ERT surveys at the same location over days or weeks. In Montreal, this is used to track grout propagation during underpinning or to monitor seasonal moisture changes under slab-on-grade foundations.

Top questions

How much does an electrical resistivity survey cost in Montreal?

A typical VES or short ERT line in the Montreal region ranges from CA$820 to CA$1,600, depending on the number of soundings, electrode array length, and site access constraints. A detailed quote is always provided after reviewing the site plan and project objectives.

How deep can VES investigate around the island of Montreal?

With a 200 m maximum current-electrode spread, the investigation depth reaches roughly 60 to 80 metres, depending on the subsurface resistivity contrast. That depth easily covers the overburden sequence and the top of the sedimentary rock beneath the Montreal metropolitan area.

Can resistivity surveys work on asphalt or concrete surfaces?

Yes, but electrode coupling must be handled carefully. We drill small pilot holes through the pavement, fill them with conductive gel, and insert the electrode. Contact resistance is checked at each station to ensure data quality meets ASTM D6431-18 criteria.

What do you deliver after the field survey?

The report includes 1D resistivity sounding curves with layer interpretation, 2D inverted resistivity sections with RMS error statistics, a site map with electrode positions, and a geotechnical interpretation correlating resistivity values to probable soil and rock types common in the Montreal region.
